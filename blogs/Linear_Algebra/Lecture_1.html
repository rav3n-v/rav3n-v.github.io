Lecture 1 — Vectors and the Birth of Vector Spaces

Posted on February 22, 2026

Introduction

In this Lecture series we will study the foundations of linear algebra. I will be following the book Linear Algebra by Stephen H. Friedberg, Arnold J. Insel, and Lawrence E. Spence. I will try to build intuition before defining abstract definitions. Helping you connect reality and the subject.

Our aim for this lecture is try to understand what are vector spaces. So we need to understand what are vectors.

Vectors vs Scalars

From physics, we know that vectors have both magnitude and direction. Examples include force, displacement, and velocity. They are quantities with that start from some point and show us direction with a specific magnitude

Scalars, on the other hand, have only magnitude. Examples include mass and temperature.

In one dimension, vectors and scalars behave similarly. In higher dimensions, distinction becomes essential. Adding of scalars is straightforward, but while adding vectors, we need to add their direction as well, therefore, we need a set of tools.

Geometric Vector Addition

If two vectors \( x \) and \( y \) act at the same point, their sum is given by the diagonal of the parallelogram with vectors as its adjacent sides.

This is known as the parallelogram law of vector addition.

Why is this true?

Vector addition is commutative. So if we add vector x to vector y, or vector y to vector x, we get the same result (think in terms of displacement). The expression x + y means we start with vector x, and then place vector y at the endpoint of x. Similarly, y + x means we start with vector y, and then place vector x at the endpoint of y (to visualize this, see the figure above). Now observe the quadrilateral formed. From Euclidean geometry, we know that it is a parallelogram, since both pairs of opposite sides are equal and parallel.

Algebraic Definition of Addition

Suppose

\[ x = (a_1, a_2), \quad y = (b_1, b_2) \]

Then their sum is defined component-wise:

\[ x + y = (a_1 + b_1,\; a_2 + b_2) \]

The geometric and algebraic definitions are equivalent.

Vectors in \( \mathbb{R}^n \)

Any vector in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) can be written as \((a_1, a_2) \: | \: a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{R} \) where \( a_1 \) and \( a_2 \) are the end points. This completely determines any vector in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \).

More generally, in \( \mathbb{R}^n \):

\[ (a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n), \quad a_i \in \mathbb{R} \]

Even though we cannot visualize high dimensions, the algebraic structure continues to hold. This why we need these algebric structures since beyond \(\mathbb{R}^3\) we cannot visualize vectors.

Scalar Multiplication

If \( t \in \mathbb{R} \) and \( x = (a_1, a_2) \), then:

\[ tx = (t a_1, t a_2) \]

Geometrically:

  • If \( |t| > 1 \), the vector stretches.
  • If \( 0 < |t| < 1 \), the vector shrinks.
  • If \( t < 0 \), the direction reverses.

Fundamental Properties

The following properties hold in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) and more generally in \( \mathbb{R}^n\), for all x, y, z in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), and a, b in \(\mathbb{R}\), we have:

\[ \begin{align*} x + y &= y + x &&\text{(Commutativity)} \\ (x + y) + z &= x + (y + z) &&\text{(Associativity)} \\ x + 0 &= x &&\text{(Additive Identity)}\\ x + (-x) &= 0 &&\text{(Additive Commutativity)}\\ 1.x &= x &&\text{(Multiplicative Identity)}\\ (ab)x &= a(bx) &&\text{(Associativity of Scalar Multiplication)}\\ a(x+y) &= ax+ay &&\text{(Distributivity of Vector Addition)}\\ (a+b)x &= ax+bx &&\text{(Distributivity of Scalar Addition)}\\ \end{align*} \]

You don't have to take my word for it. Try to break one of these in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) or in any \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Transition to Vector Spaces

Notice that nothing in the above discussion truly depended on geometry. What matters are the algebraic properties. This leads to the definition of a vector space:

A vector space is a set equipped with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying the above 8 properties.

In our case, scalars came from \( \mathbb{R} \). More generally, scalars may come from any field.
