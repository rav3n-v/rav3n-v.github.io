Taylor Series Expansion and Gradient Descent

Posted on February 2026

Introduction

Taylor Series Expansion

Any continuously differentiable function can be written as a sum involving its derivatives. Around a point \( x \), we have:

\[ f(x+h) = f(x) + h f'(x) + \frac{h^2}{2!} f''(x) + \frac{h^3}{3!} f'''(x) + \cdots \]

where \( h \) is a very small quantity, close to zero.

Since \( h \) is small, higher order terms become negligible. Keeping only the first-order term, we obtain the linear approximation:

\[ f(x+h) \approx f(x) + h f'(x) \]

Connecting to Minimization

Suppose we want to minimize a function. That means we want:

\[ f(x+h) - f(x) < 0 \]

Using the first-order approximation:

\[ f(x+h) - f(x) \approx h f'(x) \]

Therefore, to decrease the function:

\[ h f'(x) < 0 \]

We do not control the sign of \( f'(x) \), but we do control the direction of movement \( h \).

To ensure decrease, we choose:

\[ h = - f'(x) \]

That is, we move in the direction opposite to the slope of the function. This is precisely the idea behind gradient descent.
